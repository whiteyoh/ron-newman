# Glytch for Schools

![Glytch banner](assets/glytch-readme-banner.png)

Helping people understand AI beyond the chat box.

Glytch is a workshop-safe AI fluency demo that helps schools teach AI judgement, not just AI prompts.

It gives staff and students a safe way to see how AI-supported work changes when structure, evidence, checks and human review are added.

## Executive summary

Glytch helps schools introduce AI in a safe, practical and human way.

It is not about telling students to use AI for everything. It is about helping them understand what AI did, what needs checking, and where human judgement belongs.

By the end of a Glytch session, students and staff should be able to explain the difference between a quick AI answer and a checked AI-supported workflow.

## What this pack is for

Use this pack to run AI literacy sessions with:

- high school students
- sixth form students
- teachers and teaching assistants
- digital leads
- senior leadership teams
- governors
- careers and employability teams
- school support and admin teams

The same demo can be used at different depths. With students, keep the focus on learning, checking and responsible use. With staff and leaders, use it to build a shared language for safe AI adoption.

## The core message

AI should support learning, not replace thinking.

Glytch helps people understand:

- what the AI is being asked to do
- what information it is using
- what needs checking
- where human judgement belongs
- when simple help is enough
- when more structure is needed
- why more automation is not always better

## What Glytch teaches

Glytch shows that AI use is not one single thing.

A quick answer from AI is different from an answer that has used evidence, checks, review points and a clear human decision.

The simple learning model is:

Prompt → Tool → Check → Evidence → Review → Controlled work

For school use, explain it like this:

| Step | Plain English meaning |
|---|---|
| Prompt | Ask AI for help |
| Tool | Let AI use a bounded helper, such as a calculation or lookup |
| Check | Look for mistakes, weak assumptions or missing information |
| Evidence | Tie the answer to trusted material |
| Review | A person checks before using it |
| Controlled work | More steps, more checks and more responsibility |

The important lesson is:

> Use the lowest level of AI support that safely gets the job done.

## Recommended school sessions

### 20-minute taster

Use this for an assembly, short staff briefing or quick classroom introduction.

- Introduce AI beyond the chat box.
- Show a quick Level 1 answer.
- Compare it with a checked or evidence-aware level.
- Ask what changed.
- Ask what a person should still check.

### 45 to 60-minute lesson or CPD session

Use this as the recommended first school session.

- 0 to 5 minutes: explain the workshop-safe rules.
- 5 to 10 minutes: ask what people already use AI for.
- 10 to 20 minutes: run Level 1 and discuss what is missing.
- 20 to 30 minutes: run Level 3 or Level 4 and discuss tools, checks and evidence.
- 30 to 45 minutes: run Level 8 and discuss review gates and human judgement.
- 45 to 60 minutes: reflect on when AI helps learning and when it could weaken learning.

### 90-minute extended session

Use this for deeper student work, staff CPD or a pilot session.

- Run the standard 45 to 60-minute session.
- Split into small groups.
- Give each group a safe school scenario.
- Ask them to choose the lowest safe AI level for the task.
- Ask them to identify what should be checked before the output is used.
- Share back the risks, checks and review points.

## Suggested school scenarios

Use safe, generic examples. Do not use real pupil data.

Good examples:

- planning a revision timetable
- improving a paragraph for clarity
- creating quiz questions from teacher-provided notes
- comparing a quick AI answer with an evidence-based answer
- preparing questions for a debate
- creating a checklist before submitting coursework
- planning a school club activity
- writing a plain-English explanation of a difficult topic
- checking whether an AI answer has made unsupported claims

Avoid:

- personal pupil data
- safeguarding information
- health, legal or financial decisions
- behaviour records
- assessment decisions about named students
- anything that must be trusted without human review

## School-safe principles

Glytch should be used with clear safety boundaries.

During a school session:

- do not enter personal pupil data
- do not enter sensitive school data
- do not treat AI answers as automatically correct
- do not use AI output as a final decision
- do not use the demo for safeguarding decisions
- do not use the demo to replace teacher judgement
- do keep the focus on checking, evidence and review
- do remind students that AI can be useful and still be wrong

Schools should follow their own safeguarding, data protection, acceptable use and AI policies.

## How this supports AI literacy themes

Glytch is not affiliated with, endorsed by or certified by UNESCO or any other external organisation.

It is designed to support broad AI literacy themes that many schools and education bodies are already discussing:

- human-centred AI use
- critical thinking
- safe and responsible use
- understanding AI limitations
- checking evidence and assumptions
- keeping people in control of important decisions

Glytch helps make those ideas visible in a classroom or workshop setting.

## What Glytch is not

Glytch is not:

- a homework machine
- a replacement teacher
- a safeguarding system
- a production automation platform
- a fact-checking engine
- a reason to trust AI without review
- tied to one AI company or one AI product

Glytch is a safe way to explore how AI-supported work changes when more structure, evidence and review are added.

## Running a first school pilot

For a first pilot, keep it small and measurable.

Recommended approach:

- choose one class, staff group or leadership group
- use safe example scenarios
- run a 45 to 60-minute session
- collect before-and-after confidence questions
- ask what changed in people’s understanding
- record what still feels unclear
- decide whether to repeat, revise or expand the session

Use the [Pilot plan](PILOT_PLAN.md) for session formats, evidence collection, success indicators and safety boundaries.
