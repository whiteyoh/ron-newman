# Glytch Facilitator Guide

This guide helps a teacher, digital lead or workshop leader run a Glytch session with students or staff.

The aim is simple: help people understand AI beyond the chat box.

## What you are helping people learn

By the end of the session, the group should be able to:

- explain how AI help changes when you add steps and checks
- spot what needs checking before using an AI answer
- understand why evidence matters
- decide where a person still needs to review the work
- choose the lowest safe level of AI help for a task
- explain why AI should support learning, not replace thinking

## Before the session

### Check the demo

A facilitator should open the demo before the session and check that it loads.

You do not need the group to understand Python, APIs or model settings.

### Choose a scenario

Pick one simple school-friendly scenario, such as:

- plan a revision timetable
- improve a paragraph for clarity
- create quiz questions from teacher-provided notes
- compare a simple answer with an evidence-based answer
- plan a careers research task

Avoid any scenario that uses real pupil data or sensitive school information.

### Set the safety expectations

Start with these rules:

- Do not enter personal pupil data.
- Do not enter sensitive school data.
- Do not trust AI answers automatically.
- Check before using anything.
- AI should support thinking, not replace it.

## Suggested 45–60 minute flow

### 0–5 minutes: Set the idea

Say:

AI is not just a chat box. The useful skill is knowing what to ask, what to check, and when a person needs to stay in control.

### 5–10 minutes: Ask what people already do

Ask:

- Where have you seen AI used?
- What do people use it for?
- When might it help?
- When might it cause problems?

Do not let this become a long debate. Capture a few examples and move on.

### 10–20 minutes: Run Level 1

Frame Level 1 as simple AI help.

Ask:

- What did the AI give us?
- What might be useful?
- What might be wrong?
- What would you check before using it?

Key point: a quick answer is not automatically a good answer.

### 20–30 minutes: Run Level 3

Frame Level 3 as AI with a helper and a checkable step.

Ask:

- What changed compared with Level 1?
- Did this make the answer easier to trust?
- What still needs human checking?

Key point: adding a tool or check can help, but it does not remove responsibility.

### 30–40 minutes: Run Level 4 or Level 6

Use Level 4 to talk about evidence.

Use Level 6 to talk about review and improvement.

Ask:

- What information did the AI use?
- Was the answer tied to evidence?
- What changed after review?
- Which version would you trust more, and why?

Key point: better AI work is often checked work.

### 40–50 minutes: Run Level 8

Frame Level 8 as more moving parts, more need to check.

Avoid making it sound like the AI is in charge.

Ask:

- What jobs were split into parts?
- Where could mistakes still happen?
- Who should approve the final output?
- When would this be too much for a simple task?

Key point: more steps can help, but more steps also mean more responsibility.

### 50–60 minutes: Reflect

Ask the group:

- When is simple AI help enough?
- When do we need evidence?
- When do we need a human review point?
- When should we not use AI at all?

End with:

AI should support learning, not replace thinking.

## Facilitation tips

- Keep the language plain.
- Ask people what they would check.
- Do not present higher levels as automatically better.
- Keep bringing the conversation back to judgement.
- Make it clear that AI can be useful and still need checking.
- Use school examples rather than workplace automation examples.

## Common misconceptions to challenge

### “If AI sounds confident, it must be right.”

No. Confidence is not the same as accuracy.

### “Better prompts solve everything.”

No. Some tasks need evidence, checks and review.

### “More advanced AI is always better.”

No. The best AI use is the lowest level that safely gets the job done.

### “Using AI means doing less thinking.”

No. Good AI use often needs more judgement, not less.

## Good closing message

Glytch is not here to tell us to trust AI more. It is here to help us understand what AI is doing, what needs checking, and where people still need to make the decision.
