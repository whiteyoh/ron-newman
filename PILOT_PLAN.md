# Glytch Pilot Plan

![Glytch banner](assets/glytch-readme-banner.png)

Helping people understand AI beyond the chat box.


## Executive summary

This pilot tests whether Glytch improves practical AI judgement.

It measures whether people leave with a clearer understanding of:

- what the AI did
- what evidence was used
- what needs checking
- where human review belongs
- why more automation is not automatically better

The pilot is designed to be safe, measurable and easy to run with a school, team, organisation or workshop group.

This pilot plan is designed to help a school, team, organisation or workshop group test Glytch safely and gather evidence about whether it improves AI confidence, AI judgement and understanding of human review.

Glytch is a workshop-safe simulation. It does not take real-world action, change files, send messages, make decisions for people or remove the need for human review.

## 1. Purpose of the pilot

The purpose of the pilot is to test whether Glytch helps people understand how AI use changes when a task moves from a simple prompt to a more structured, checked and reviewable workflow.

The pilot should answer four questions:

- Do people better understand the difference between a quick AI answer and a checked AI-supported answer?
- Do people become more confident identifying when AI output needs review?
- Do people understand why evidence, assumptions and checks matter?
- Can people explain where human judgement should remain in control?

The pilot is not intended to prove that AI should be used everywhere. It is intended to test whether Glytch improves practical AI judgement.

## 2. Target audience

The pilot can be run with one audience or compared across multiple audiences.

Suitable pilot groups include:

- high school or sixth form students
- teachers and school support staff
- senior leadership teams
- public sector teams
- non-technical managers
- small business owners
- early careers groups
- community learning groups
- teams preparing for AI adoption

Recommended first pilot size:

- 8 to 20 people for a workshop pilot
- 20 to 40 people for a larger classroom or staff-session pilot

## 3. Session format

### 30-minute taster

Use this when time is limited.

- 5 minutes: introduce the idea of AI beyond the chat box
- 5 minutes: run a simple Level 1 example
- 10 minutes: compare with Level 3 or Level 4
- 5 minutes: show Level 8 orchestration
- 5 minutes: capture feedback and confidence scores

### 45 to 60-minute pilot session

Use this as the recommended pilot format.

- 5 minutes: explain Glytch and the workshop-safe rules
- 10 minutes: collect baseline confidence questions
- 10 minutes: run Level 1 and discuss what is missing
- 10 minutes: run Level 3 or Level 4 and discuss checks and evidence
- 10 minutes: run Level 8 and discuss review gates and human judgement
- 5 to 10 minutes: collect final confidence questions and feedback

### 90-minute extended session

Use this for deeper learning or staff CPD.

- Run the 45 to 60-minute pilot session
- Add group discussion using different scenarios
- Ask each group to choose the lowest safe AI level for a task
- Ask each group to identify what should be checked before use
- Capture examples, risks and follow-up actions

## 4. What Glytch will demonstrate

The pilot should show that AI work can be understood at different levels:

| Level | What people see | What it teaches |
|---:|---|---|
| 1 | A quick AI answer | Fast output still needs judgement |
| 3 | A bounded tool-style path | Tools and checks reduce guesswork |
| 4 | A more evidence-aware answer | Grounding makes checking easier |
| 6 | Critique and revision | First drafts should not always be final |
| 8 | Simulated orchestration | More automation needs more visible review |

The key lesson is:

> Use the lowest level of AI support that safely gets the job done.

## 5. Suggested pilot scenarios

Choose scenarios that are safe, generic and easy to understand.

Good pilot examples:

- prepare a customer update from confirmed facts and assumptions
- create a simple project plan for a small team
- write a plain-English explanation of a complex topic
- compare a quick answer with an evidence-based answer
- prepare a checklist before sending an important communication
- plan a community event with risks and checks
- create a briefing note for non-technical readers

Avoid:

- personal data
- sensitive school or business data
- medical, legal or financial decisions
- real incident details that are not already approved for sharing
- anything that requires the AI output to be trusted without review

## 6. Before-and-after confidence questions

Ask these questions before and after the session.

Use a 1 to 5 scale:

1 = strongly disagree  
5 = strongly agree

| Question | Before | After |
|---|---:|---:|
| I understand the difference between a quick AI answer and a checked AI-supported answer. | 1–5 | 1–5 |
| I know when AI output needs human review. | 1–5 | 1–5 |
| I can explain why evidence and assumptions matter when using AI. | 1–5 | 1–5 |
| I understand why more automation is not always better. | 1–5 | 1–5 |
| I feel more confident using AI safely and responsibly. | 1–5 | 1–5 |
| I can explain where a person should stay in control. | 1–5 | 1–5 |

After the session, also ask:

- What changed in your understanding of AI?
- What part of Glytch made the biggest difference?
- What still feels unclear?
- What would you want to try next?
- Where could this be useful in your school, team or organisation?

## 7. Evidence to collect

Collect both numbers and comments.

### Quantitative evidence

- number of people attending
- before-and-after confidence scores
- average confidence increase per question
- percentage of people who improved by at least one point
- most improved question
- least improved question

### Qualitative evidence

- short participant comments
- examples of changed understanding
- questions people asked during the session
- common risks or concerns raised
- facilitator observations
- suggested use cases from the group

### Optional evidence

- screenshots of anonymised Glytch runs
- examples of before/after output comparisons
- group notes about where human review belongs
- anonymised quotes for a pilot summary

## 8. Success criteria

A pilot should be considered successful if most of the following are true:

- average confidence improves across the before-and-after questions
- people can explain why AI output needs checking
- people can describe the difference between a prompt and a workflow
- people can identify where human review belongs
- people understand that higher automation is not automatically better
- the session produces useful feedback for improving Glytch
- no personal or sensitive data is used during the pilot

Suggested strong-result indicators:

- at least 70% of attendees improve by one point or more on at least three questions
- at least 80% of attendees agree that Glytch made AI checks and review easier to understand
- clear qualitative feedback showing that people now think differently about trusting AI output

## 9. Safeguards and limitations

Glytch should be introduced with clear safety boundaries.

During the pilot:

- do not enter personal data
- do not enter sensitive organisational data
- do not treat AI output as automatically correct
- do not use the demo to make real decisions
- do not imply that Glytch is a production automation platform
- do not imply that Glytch is a fact-checking engine
- do not imply external endorsement unless formally confirmed

Glytch demonstrates AI judgement. It does not replace it.

## 10. Pilot roles

Recommended roles for a pilot session:

| Role | Responsibility |
|---|---|
| Facilitator | Runs the session and explains each level |
| Observer | Captures questions, concerns and feedback |
| Technical owner | Confirms the demo works before the session |
| Sponsor | Reviews the pilot evidence and decides next steps |
| Safeguarding/data owner, if needed | Confirms the session uses safe, non-sensitive examples |

For a small pilot, one person can cover more than one role.

## 12. Recommended next step after pilot

After one pilot session, decide whether to:

- improve the workshop materials
- run a second pilot with a different audience
- create a school-specific version
- create a workplace/team version
- collect more structured evidence
- prepare a short briefing for leadership

The ideal next step is a second pilot with a different audience. That shows whether Glytch works beyond one group and helps identify which audience benefits most.

## One-line summary

Glytch pilots should prove whether people leave with better AI judgement: what the AI did, what needs checking, and where human review belongs.
